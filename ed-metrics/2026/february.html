💡
Operational Insights
🚨 Insight 1 · Volume
February Had the Highest Per-Day Volume of Any Month — 160.8 Patients/Weekday Despite Being the Shortest Month
With only 28 days, February's 4,318 visits translate to 154.2 patients/day — the highest daily average of the 3-month reporting period. The weekday average of 160.8 patients/day exceeded March (155.8) and April (147.7). Mondays averaged 172.2 and never dipped below 165. This means 18 of 20 weekdays (90%) qualified as high-volume days, making February the most operationally demanding month on a per-shift basis, despite appearing smaller by total volume than March.
✅ Insight 2 · Efficiency
Best Discharge LOS of the Quarter Despite Highest Per-Day Volume — A Genuine Operations Win
February's median discharge LOS of 207 minutes (3h 27m) is the best of the 3-month period — lower than March (216 min) and April (220 min) — yet occurred during the highest-volume month by daily rate. This inverse relationship between volume and LOS suggests February's throughput efficiency was meaningfully better. Identifying what was operationally different in February (staffing mix, fast-track utilization, early disposition planning) could provide replicable lessons for future high-volume months.
⚠️ Insight 3 · LWOT Pattern
Unusual Late-Night LWOT Spike at 21:00 — 11 Events in a Single Hour
February's LWOT distribution shows a striking spike at 21:00 with 11 total events — the single largest hourly LWOT count across all 3 months combined. The broader evening window (20:00–23:00) accounts for 24 of 60 LWOT events (40%). In contrast, March and April showed LWOT spread more evenly across evening hours. This late-night clustering warrants investigation — possible causes include extended wait times during evening shift transitions, inadequate provider coverage after 20:00, or patients presenting in non-urgent states after normal care hours who de-escalate their urgency.
⚠️ Insight 4 · Provider LOS
HUNTER J Exceeds 300-Min Goal for Second Consecutive Month — Pattern Requires Direct Coaching
HUNTER J averaged 302.3 minutes in February (n=141), exceeding the 300-minute goal. This is the second consecutive month with HUNTER J in the upper tertile. Combined with BATIZY L's 304.1 min in March, the pattern of providers approaching or breaching the goal warrants an urgent but constructive peer review. The lowest-LOS provider this month is BUNKER C at 136.5 minutes — a 165-minute gap between best and highest, the widest spread of the 3-month period.
🔮 Insight 5 · Staffing Trigger
Noon Trigger Achieves Perfect Precision in February — PPV 100% With Zero False Positives
The ≥45-patients-by-noon trigger produced zero false positives in February — every alert correctly identified a high-volume day. With 94% sensitivity, it missed only 1 of 18 high-volume weekdays. Combined with March (PPV 93%) and April (PPV 91%), the 3-month evidence base is now strong enough to formalize a ≥50-patients-by-noon standing protocol. This threshold is robust across all 3 months and reflects the department's sustained high-volume pattern.
📊 Insight 6 · 3-Month Trend Summary
Q1 2026 Trend Lines: Volume Rising, Boarding Worsening, LOS Improving — A Mixed Picture
Across February, March, and April 2026, the clearest trends are:
Volume: Feb 154.2 → Mar 149.0 → Apr 143.7 avg/day (Feb was peak intensity; March highest total)
Admission LOS: Feb 368 → Mar 386 → Apr 371 min (boarding worsening in March specifically)
DC LOS: Feb 207 → Mar 216 → Apr 220 min (slight upward creep — worth watching)
ESI 2 timeliness: Feb 85.0% → Mar 83.0% → Apr 88.3% (March was the low point)
LWOT: 1.4% → 1.6% → 1.4% (stable and acceptable)
The biggest concern trending into Q2: boarding LOS and its relationship to weekday volume surge on Mondays.

Recommended Actions
1
Formalize the ≥50-Noon Trigger as a Standing Protocol: With 3 months of validated data (PPV 91–100%), this threshold is ready for operationalization. Draft a 1-page protocol document for TeamHealth: at noon each weekday, charge RN checks cumulative arrivals; if ≥50, attending is notified within 15 minutes and supplemental coverage discussion is initiated. Present to leadership with the 3-month PPV table.
2
Investigate What Made February Discharge LOS the Best Month: February's 207-min median DC LOS during the highest daily volume is anomalous in a good way. Review shift schedules, provider mix, fast-track utilization, and disposition timing from February vs March and April. Identify the operational variables that drove efficiency and attempt to replicate them.
3
Targeted Coaching for HUNTER J — Second Consecutive Month Above Goal Threshold: HUNTER J is the only provider approaching or exceeding 300 min in multiple consecutive months. Schedule a direct, constructive 1:1 conversation with peer LOS benchmarks and a specific improvement goal for Q2. Frame around the 300-min departmental standard and the 165-minute gap between best and highest performers.
4
Audit the 21:00 LWOT Spike: 11 LWOT events in one hour and 40% of monthly LWOT between 20:00–23:00 is unusual. Pull the specific dates and circumstances of these events. Determine whether this reflects an after-hours staffing gap, a documentation issue, or a patient population characteristic on specific nights.
5
Present Q1 2026 Boarding Trend to Hospital Leadership: Boarding LOS climbed from 368 to 386 to 371 across the quarter — not improving. Combined with consistently high weekday volume (147–161/day), this represents a systemic capacity mismatch. Present the 3-month admit LOS trend with a proposal for a formal ED-to-inpatient throughput initiative, including bed assignment SLA enforcement and an escalation protocol for patients boarding >4 hours.
